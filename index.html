Cartographic Reflection

The first map from the beginning of the course is actually not bad, although it may require some zooming in and out in some areas. The choice of font and the map title are probably its strongest elements, as I think the title describes the content quite well and does so in a visually pleasant way. I will not even go too deeply into the scale bar, as it is rather unfortunate. At the time, I clearly had no idea where to place it.

The data presentation works better in the outer areas of Helsinki, where the districts are larger and the building years tend to be more similar within each district. In the central parts of Helsinki, however, the map becomes somewhat cluttered. The districts are smaller there, and presenting the average construction year for each district becomes more distracting than informative. The district boundaries also work better in the outer parts of Helsinki, while in the centre they are not always clearly visible. On the other hand, they were never meant to be the main focus of the map. The idea was to keep them subtle, while still making it possible to understand where the district borders are.

I am still not completely sure how well the heatmap behind the buildings works. In some ways, it looks a bit clumsy, but on the other hand, it does smooth out the variation within each district quite well. It also gives a nicer overview of the area than the building colours alone. The colour scale chosen for the buildings is probably not the best. I am not sure if the colours themselves are bad, but against the dark background they create a slightly spooky, almost Halloween-like impression. I am also not fully sure why I originally chose a dark theme, as it makes the map feel somewhat aggressive. The main colour of the heatmap is slightly yellowish, and for that reason the dark background works fairly well, since yellow can easily disappear on a white background. Nevertheless, it might have been better to change the colours of the heatmap and buildings instead of changing the entire background to a dark one. At the time, however, I felt that dark-themed maps looked cool, and I still think they often do.

The Data and Methods section could be smaller and should have more breathing room, as it now feels slightly overstated. Still, the information itself is concise and informative. The north arrow is also far too emphasized and becomes an unnecessary eye-catcher. The legend is quite basic, although I still think it was a good idea to categorize the buildings by construction decade.

Description of data

For the data description, I will start with why the topic interested me in the first place. I am a big fan of football, and with the World Cup coming up, it immediately felt like a good idea to make something out of it. The 2026 World Cup is larger than previous tournaments in several ways. It has more teams than ever before, but it is also spatially much larger, as the tournament is spread across Canada, the United States and Mexico. I had also read news articles about the long distances teams will need to travel between matches, so the topic felt both interesting and cartographically promising. I thought there could be some visually strong maps about the enormous size of the tournament and the almost ridiculous distances the teams are required to travel.

When I started looking for a dataset that would cover the required information, the first useful result led me to the OpenFootball GitHub account. The repository included files from all previous World Cups, as well as a folder for the 2026 tournament. This folder contained six JSON files: worldcup.groups.json, worldcup.json, worldcup.quali_playoffs.json, worldcup.squads.json, worldcup.stadiums.json and worldcup.teams.json.

Of these files, I mainly used worldcup.json, worldcup.teams.json and worldcup.stadiums.json. The worldcup.json file contained information about each match, including the teams, match location and time. The stadiums file included information about the stadiums, such as their location, construction year and other stadium-related attributes. The teams file was mainly used to identify the confederation from which each team had qualified for the World Cup.

Additional datasets were used for cartographic background and visual context. From Natural Earth Data, I used country boundaries and time zone polygons. I also used SVG flag files from GitHub user hampusborgos to include the flags of the participating countries.

Geospatial Narrative

The Contestants

The first map shows the global distribution of qualified teams and how the 48-team tournament is divided between FIFA confederations. The countries are coloured by FIFA confederation, which makes it possible to see both the worldwide reach of the tournament and the regional structure behind the qualification process. The map also highlights the expanded 48-team format, where Europe still has by far the largest number of teams, but the tournament includes a broad selection of countries from every inhabited continent.

Instead of labelling all countries directly on the map, I placed the team names and flags in a separate panel. Ofcourse, it requires some knowledge from the observer to know where those countries actually locate on the map, but still, it fel like a good idea. This keeps the map itself cleaner, especially in dense areas such as Europe, Southern- and Central America. The map therefore works more as a global overview, while the panel provides the exact list of participating teams. And since I have a soft spot for country flags, they were going to make an appearance whether they had a strictly necessary cartographic role or not. Overall, the first map is intended to introduce the topic and show the global coverage of the World Cup.

The Stadiums

The second map focuses on the spatial setting of the tournament by showing the 16 host stadiums across Canada, the United States and Mexico. The stadium points are coloured by host country, which makes it easier to see how the venues are distributed between the three organisers. I also included time zone polygons in the background, because they help communicate the continental scale of the tournament without adding too much visual noise.

The two inset maps highlight MetLife Stadium and Estadio Azteca, as they represent the final and the opening match. Initially, the idea was to present all stadiums in detail as there were plenty of empty space, but that was a stupid idea. It works better with only two insets, but how well, I am not sure. Overall, the map is meant to show that the 2026 World Cup is not only larger in terms of teams, but also geographically much larger than a typical single-country tournament.

And yes, looking at the map now, I can see that the stadium symbols are somewhat misleading. The visual difference between the smallest and largest points is quite large, even though the actual difference in stadium capacity is not that dramatic.
